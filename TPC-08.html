Ch. 8: General Defenses to Criminal Responsibility

Back to Main Page

Sec. 8.01. Insanity

  • This is an affirmative defense.
  • Test: At the time of the conduct, the actor, as a result of a severe mental disease or defect, did not know that his conduct was wrong.
  • Exclusion: "Mental disease or defect" does *not* include an abnormality manifested *only* by repeated criminal or otherwise antisocial conduct.

Sec. 8.02. Mistake of Fact

  • This is a defense (not affirmative).
  • Test: The actor, through mistake, formed a reasonable belief about a matter of fact.
  • Effect: This mistaken belief must have negated the kind of culpability required for the offense (e.g., they didn't act "knowingly").
  • Lesser Offense: The actor may still be convicted of any lesser included offense they would be guilty of if the fact were as they believed.

Sec. 8.03. Mistake of Law

  • General Rule (a): Ignorance of the law is no defense.
  • Exception (b): It is an affirmative defense if the actor reasonably believed the conduct was not a crime AND acted in reasonable reliance upon:
    1. An official written statement or permission from an administrative agency; OR
    2. A written interpretation from a court of record or a public official responsible for interpreting that law.
  • Lesser Offense (c): The actor may still be convicted of a lesser included offense they would be guilty of if the law were as they believed.

Sec. 8.04. Intoxication

  • (a): Voluntary intoxication is NOT a defense to the commission of a crime.
  • (b): Evidence of temporary insanity caused by intoxication may be introduced in mitigation of the penalty (i.e., at the punishment phase).
  • (c): If temporary insanity from intoxication is relied on, the court *must* charge the jury accordingly.
  • (d): "Intoxication" means disturbance of mental or physical capacity from introducing *any* substance into the body.

Sec. 8.05. Duress

  • This is an affirmative defense.
  • Test (a) (Felonies): The actor was compelled by threat of imminent death or serious bodily injury to himself or another.
  • Test (b) (Non-Felonies): The actor was compelled by force or threat of force.
  • (c): Compulsion only exists if the force/threat would render a person of reasonable firmness incapable of resisting.
  • Defense Unavailable (d): The defense is lost if the actor intentionally, knowingly, or recklessly placed himself in the situation where compulsion was probable.
  • (e): Acting at a spouse's command is not a defense, unless it meets the compulsion standards above.

Sec. 8.06. Entrapment

  • This is a defense (not affirmative).
  • Test (a): The actor was induced by a law enforcement agent using persuasion or other means likely to cause persons (plural) to commit the offense.
  • Exclusion (a): Conduct merely affording an opportunity to commit an offense is *not* entrapment.
  • (b): "Law enforcement agent" includes state, local, and U.S. personnel, and any person acting on their instructions.

Sec. 8.07. Age Affecting Criminal Responsibility

  • (a) Younger than 15: Cannot be prosecuted or convicted for offenses committed when < 15, *except* for:
    1. Perjury/Aggravated Perjury (if they had sufficient discretion).
    2. Certain Transportation Code violations.
    3. Motor vehicle traffic ordinances.
    4. Misdemeanors punishable by fine only.
    5. Violations of penal ordinances.
    6. Serious felonies (Capital, Agg. Controlled Substance, F1) if 14+ and transferred via Family Code 54.02.
    7. Capital Felony or Murder if transferred via specific statute (FC 54.02(j)(2)(A)).
  • (b) Younger than 17 (General Rule): Cannot be prosecuted or convicted for an offense committed *before* age 17, *unless* the juvenile court waives jurisdiction (or has previously). This does *not* apply to the offenses listed in (a)(1)-(5).
  • (c) Younger than 18: No person can be punished by death for an offense committed when < 18.
  • (d) Younger than 10: Cannot be prosecuted for fine-only misdemeanors or penal ordinances ((a)(4) or (5)) committed when < 10.
  • (e) Age 10 to 14: For fine-only/ordinance offenses ((a)(4) or (5)), a child 10-14 is presumed incapable of committing the offense.
    • The prosecution can rebut this by a preponderance of the evidence, proving the child had sufficient capacity to know the *conduct was wrong*.
    • The state does *not* have to prove the child knew it was a criminal offense or knew the legal consequences.

Sec. 8.08. Child With Mental Illness, Disability, or Lack of Capacity

  • Applies to: Offenses described in 8.07(a)(4) and (5) (fine-only misdemeanors and penal ordinances).
  • (a) Court Determination: On motion or its own, the court shall determine if probable cause exists to believe the child:
    1. Is unfit to proceed (lacks capacity to understand proceedings or assist defense); OR
    2. Lacks substantial capacity to appreciate wrongfulness or conform conduct to the law.
  • (b): If the court finds probable cause for (a)(1) or (a)(2), it may dismiss the complaint.
  • (c): A dismissal under (b) may be appealed as provided by Art. 44.01, CCP.

Note: Per the statute, Sec. 8.09 is listed as becoming effective on December 4, 2025.

Sec. 8.09. Victim of Trafficking or Compelling Prostitution

  • (a) Test: It is an affirmative defense that the actor:
    1. Was a victim of Trafficking (20A.02) or Compelling Prostitution (43.05); AND
    2. Engaged in the charged conduct as a direct result of being *caused* to do so by force, fraud, or coercion.
  • (b) Limitations: The defense *only* applies if:
    1. The actor would *not* have engaged in the conduct "but for" the force, fraud, or coercion;
    2. The force, fraud, or coercion would have compelled a reasonable person in the actor's circumstances;
    3. The actor was *not* merely afforded an opportunity; AND
    4. The conduct is *not* a serious (Art. 42A.054(a)) offense, *unless* the actor is charged only as a party.
  • (c): "Force, fraud, or coercion" has the same meaning as in Sec. 20A.02.
  • (d): Information relevant to identifying the defendant as a victim may be offered to establish the defense.
  • (e) No Proof Needed: The actor is not required to prove that the person using force, fraud, or coercion was ever charged with or convicted of the trafficking offense.
