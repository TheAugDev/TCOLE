Ch. 35: Insurance Fraud

Back to Main Page

Key Definitions (Sec. 35.01)

Insurance policy

A written instrument providing terms of insurance. Broadly defined to include certificates, binders, contracts, benefit plans, surety bonds, cash bonds, or any other alternative to insurance authorized by law (e.g., Trans. Code 601).

Insurer

Has the meaning assigned by Article 1.02, Insurance Code.

Statement

An oral or written communication or a record/documented representation of fact made to an insurer. Includes computer-generated information.

Value of the claim

The total dollar amount of a claim for payment, or as applicable, the value determined under Sec. 35.025.

Sec. 35.015. Materiality

A statement is material (regardless of admissibility at trial) if it could have affected:

  • The eligibility for coverage or amount of payment on a claim; OR
  • The decision of an insurer whether to issue an insurance policy.

Sec. 35.02. Insurance Fraud

Culpable Mental State: Intent to defraud or deceive an insurer.

Offenses:

  • Offense (a) (Fraudulent Claim): In support of a claim, a person (1) prepares or (2) presents a statement they know contains false or misleading material information.
  • Offense (a-1) (Fraudulent Application): In support of an application, a person (1) prepares or (2) presents a statement they know contains false or misleading material information.
    • Penalty (d): This offense is *always* a State Jail Felony.
  • Offense (b) (Kickbacks): Solicits, offers, pays, or receives a benefit in connection with furnishing goods or services for which a claim is submitted.

Penalties (for Offenses (a) and (b)):

  • < $100: Class C Misdemeanor
  • $100 < $750: Class B Misdemeanor
  • $750 < $2,500: Class A Misdemeanor
  • $2,500 < $30,000: State Jail Felony
  • $30,000 < $150,000: Felony of the Third Degree
  • $150,000 < $300,000: Felony of the Second Degree
  • $300,000 or more: Felony of the First Degree
  • SPECIAL ENHANCEMENT (c)(7)(B): It is also a Felony of the First Degree if an act committed in connection with the offense places a person at risk of death or serious bodily injury.

Other Provisions:

  • Restitution (e): Court shall order restitution, including court costs and attorney's fees.
  • Valid Loss (g): If actor proves by a preponderance of the evidence that a portion of the claim was valid, the value = (Total Claim) - (Valid Portion).
  • Presumption (h): If actor submitted a bill, a rebuttable presumption exists they caused the claim to be prepared/presented.

Sec. 35.025. Value of Claim

  • When Not Ascertainable (a): If value is not clear, it is the fair market value of the goods/services OR the cost of replacing them.
  • When Impossible to Ascertain (b): If value *still* can't be reasonably ascertained, it is considered to be $750 or more but less than $2,500 (a Class A Misdemeanor level).

Sec. 35.03. Aggregation and Multiple Offenses

  • Aggregation (a): When separate claims are part of one scheme or continuing course of conduct, the conduct is one offense and the values are aggregated.
  • Enhancement (b): When three or more separate claims are part of one scheme, the conduct is one offense, and the classification is one category higher than the most serious single claim. (F1 stays F1).
  • Exclusivity: The state must choose one. You cannot use both (a) and (b) at the same time.
